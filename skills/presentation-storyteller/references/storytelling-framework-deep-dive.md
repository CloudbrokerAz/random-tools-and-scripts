## 1. The Cotton Candy vs. Best Meal Test

An anecdote is cotton candy — delicious in the moment, forgotten immediately. A story is the best meal of your life — you remember the restaurant, who you were with, what you ordered.

When reviewing a presentation draft, test each section: is this cotton candy or a meal? Cotton candy moments aren't wrong — humor, quick anecdotes, fun data points all serve a purpose as connective tissue. But the core beats must be meals. The audience should be thinking about the key moments for days, weeks, possibly their career.

The test: "Will the audience want to repeat this to someone else?" If yes, it's a meal. If not, it might still serve the deck as a transition or palate cleanser, but it shouldn't be a core beat.

## 2. Scene Construction Details

Every scene in a presentation needs:
- **A location/moment** — ground the audience in a specific where and when
- **An action** — something is happening, not just being described
- **A connection** — but/therefore linking to the next scene

What a scene does NOT need:
- Exhaustive description (the audience fills in details from their own experience)
- Character introductions before they're relevant (introduce people at the moment they matter)
- Context that can be woven in later

Example of a bad scene opening for a product launch:
> "Our company was founded in 2015 by three engineers who met at Stanford. We started in enterprise SaaS and pivoted to developer tools in 2018. Our team is now 150 people across three offices."

Example of a good scene opening:
> "It's 3 AM on a Tuesday. Our Slack channel lights up. Three of our biggest customers are reporting the same error. And we don't know why."

The second version has location (late night, Slack channel), action (crisis unfolding), and creates immediate stakes. Everything the audience needs to know about the company can be woven in as the story demands it.

## 3. The Not/Opposite Technique

Describing something by what it's NOT creates energy and contrast. It presents a dichotomy that activates the audience's mind:

- "She's not stupid" hits differently than "She's smart" — it presents both possibilities
- "This wasn't an engineering problem" creates more curiosity than "This was a sales problem"
- "We didn't need to move faster" is more compelling than "We needed to change direction"

Use this technique on:
- Key insight slides (the moment of realization)
- Transition points (shifting from one phase to another)
- The opening (what this presentation is NOT about)
- Anywhere you want the audience to feel the weight of a choice

Practice pattern: [Thing] wasn't [expected category]. It was [surprising category].

## 4. Time Compression Strategies

Long timelines kill presentations. A "company journey" spanning 10 years told chronologically is torture. Compress using:

**The BABC structure:**
- Start in a charged present moment (B)
- Flash back to origin only as far as needed (A)
- Return to the present with new context (B)
- Move forward to resolution/future (C)

**The montage slide:**
When you need to cover a stretch of time (multiple quarters, several product iterations), use a single montage slide or quick sequence — 3 specific examples that represent the pattern, chosen for variety:
- Different types of situations
- Different scales
- Different emotional tones

Three specific examples are better than a timeline graphic. The audience infers the pattern from the examples.

**The time-jump surprise:**
When you can, hide the time span. Let the audience discover "wait, this has been going on for three years?" as a reveal rather than stating it upfront. The surprise of duration amplifies significance.

## 5. Handling Boring-But-Necessary Content

Every presentation has sections that must exist but aren't inherently engaging — compliance data, technical specs, methodology, financial details. Two strategies:

**Strategy 1: Suspense wrapper**
Create a question or mystery before the boring section that can only be resolved by the data within it. "Before I show you the numbers, I want you to guess: what percentage of users actually completed onboarding?" Now the data slide has stakes — they want to verify their prediction.

**Strategy 2: Character frame**
Instead of presenting features/data abstractly, run them through a character. "Remember Sarah from the logistics company? Watch what happens when she opens the new dashboard..." Now the demo has a human story running through it. The audience cares about Sarah's outcome, not the feature list.

Never just present boring content and hope the audience endures it. If you can't make it engaging, question whether it needs to be there at all. Can it be in an appendix? A handout? A follow-up email?

## 6. From Abstract to Specific: Business Deck Patterns

Common business presentation types and how to apply narrative structure:

**Quarterly Business Review:**
- Elephant: The one metric/trend that defines this quarter
- Don't: Walk through every metric sequentially
- Do: Tell the story of the quarter through the lens of one central challenge or insight
- Close with: A crystal ball prediction for next quarter

**Product Launch:**
- Elephant: The problem that exists in the world right now (not your solution)
- Don't: Start with features
- Do: Start with the human frustration your product eliminates
- Use the backpack: Load the audience with how things currently work (badly) before revealing the fix

**Investor Pitch:**
- Elephant: The gap in the market (stated as a human problem, not a market size)
- Don't: Start with TAM/SAM/SOM
- Do: Start with one person's story that represents the million
- The arc: "This person struggled → we built this → now their life is different → imagine that times a million"

**Internal Strategy:**
- Elephant: What happens if we don't change
- Don't: Start with the recommendation
- Do: Start with the problem felt at the ground level (a specific team, customer, or moment)
- Use breadcrumbs: Plant clues about your solution before revealing it

## 7. Authentic Delivery Principles

When writing speaker notes, optimize for spoken language, not written language:

- **Drop subjects**: "Went to the customer. Saw the problem firsthand." hits harder than "I went to the customer. I saw the problem firsthand."
- **Sentence fragments are good**: They create punch. They create rhythm.
- **Don't script humor**: Note where humor fits ("lighthearted aside about X") but don't write jokes. Written jokes delivered aloud sound wooden.
- **Include pause markers**: [pause] before key reveals. The hourglass technique — when the audience wants the next thing most, make them wait one beat longer.
- **Flag flexibility points**: Mark sections as "expandable" or "cuttable" so the speaker can read the room and adjust.

The goal is speaker notes that sound like a person talking to another person — not a document being read aloud. Imperfection is beauty. Spontaneity is trust.

## 8. Common Presentation Archetypes

Most compelling presentations follow one of these narrative shapes:

**The Discovery Arc**: "We didn't know → We investigated → We found something surprising → It changed how we think"

**The Against-All-Odds Arc**: "Everything was against us → We tried and failed → We found an unlikely solution → It worked (but not how we expected)"

**The Hidden-in-Plain-Sight Arc**: "This ordinary thing → was actually extraordinary → and here's what it means for all of us"

**The Reckoning Arc**: "We were wrong about something fundamental → Here's what we learned → Here's what we're doing about it"

**The Convergence Arc**: "These separate threads → seemed unrelated → but they connect in a way that reveals something bigger"

Match the archetype to the user's content and truth. Don't force a story shape that doesn't fit the reality — audiences can sense inauthenticity instantly. The right structure makes the truth more vivid, not more fictional.
